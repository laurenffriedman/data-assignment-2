People Who Suffer the Most From Migraines May Be Less Likely to Get Treatment

Migraines are common, affecting nearly 1 in 6 Americans. But many of the people who experience them have their pain dismissed or undertreated.

By Lauren F. Friedman / November 7, 2022

By most accounts, people have been plagued by the debilitating pain of migraine headaches for thousands of years. But unlike the many diseases that have been eliminated by vaccines and better sanitation, migraines are still very much with us—especially some of us.

Migraines involve not only pain, which can last for hours or days, but also often nausea, vomiting, and a heightened sensitivity to light and sound. They are among the most common neurological disorders, affecting nearly 1 in 6 U.S. adults, according to data from the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention.

Women have long been much more likely to experience migraines than men. Some research suggests that estrogen itself may be a potent trigger, with many women at an elevated risk for a migraine at a particular point in their menstrual cycle. But women are also less likely to be properly treated for all kinds of pain, with research showing that physicians are less likely to thoroughly investigate the cause of women’s pain and more likely to dismiss it as psychological in origin.

Migraines are also much more common among people living in poverty; people making less than $35,000 a year are 57 percent more likely to experience migraines than people making $100,000 or more.

The reasons why are complicated and not fully understood. Though it's possible that the stresses of poverty could trigger migraines, an alternate explanation is plausible too, writes Rebecca Burch, a neurologist at the Graham Headache Center at Harvard Medical School, in an analysis of the CDC data: "Given the well-recognized disability associated with migraine it is also possible that migraine may interfere with completion of education and maintenance of employment."

What's more, research has found that people without insurance are less likely to receive appropriate treatment for migraines and more likely to receive care in the emergency department, rather than from a primary care provider or a specialist. The disparities in migraine treatment may be especially acute now, Burch writes, as more effective—and much more expensive—treatments for migraine have recently become available.
